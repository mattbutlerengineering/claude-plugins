# Before & After Examples

Real examples of improved UX copy. Each example includes the problem, the improvement, and the reasoning behind the change.

## Buttons & CTAs

### Example 1: Generic Button Text

**Before**: "OK"

**After**: "Save changes"

**Why it's better**:
- Specific action instead of vague acknowledgment
- Users know exactly what will happen
- More actionable and clear

---

### Example 2: Redundant Button Text

**Before**: "Click here to submit form"

**After**: "Submit"

**Why it's better**:
- "Click here" is redundant (users know buttons are clickable)
- "Submit" is more concise
- Alternatively: "Create account" or "Send message" (be specific about what you're submitting)

---

### Example 3: Vague Secondary Action

**Before**: "Cancel"

**After**: "Keep editing" or "Go back"

**Why it's better**:
- "Cancel" doesn't tell users where they'll go
- Specific outcome is clearer
- Reduces user anxiety about losing work

---

### Example 4: Long Button Text

**Before**: "Click this button to continue to the next page"

**After**: "Continue"

**Why it's better**:
- Dramatically more concise (1 word vs 9)
- "Click this button" is unnecessary
- "Continue" is actionable and clear

---

## Error Messages

### Example 5: Technical Error Message

**Before**: "Error 404: Resource not found"

**After**: "We couldn't find that page. Check the URL or return to the homepage."

**Why it's better**:
- Plain language instead of technical jargon
- Explains what happened in human terms
- Provides actionable next steps

---

### Example 6: Blaming Error Message

**Before**: "You entered an invalid email address"

**After**: "This email format isn't recognized. Please use a format like name@example.com"

**Why it's better**:
- Doesn't blame the user
- Provides an example of correct format
- Helpful and instructive

---

### Example 7: Vague Error

**Before**: "Invalid input"

**After**: "Password must be at least 8 characters and include a number"

**Why it's better**:
- Specific about what's wrong
- Tells users exactly how to fix it
- Reduces frustration and trial-and-error

---

### Example 8: Unhelpful Network Error

**Before**: "Connection failed"

**After**: "We couldn't connect to the server. Check your internet connection and try again."

**Why it's better**:
- Explains what happened
- Suggests likely cause (internet connection)
- Provides clear next step (try again)

---

### Example 9: System Error Without Context

**Before**: "500 Internal Server Error"

**After**: "Something went wrong on our end. We're looking into it. Please try again in a few minutes."

**Why it's better**:
- User-friendly language
- Takes responsibility ("on our end")
- Sets expectation for when to retry
- Reassures users it's being handled

---

## Form Labels & Fields

### Example 10: Formal Form Label

**Before**: "First Name:"

**After**: "First name"

**Why it's better**:
- Sentence case is more modern and friendly
- No colon needed (cleaner)
- Matches contemporary UI standards

---

### Example 11: Redundant Required Indicator

**Before**: "Email Address (Required)"

**After**: "Email address *"

**Why it's better**:
- More concise
- Standard convention (asterisk = required)
- Cleaner visual design

---

### Example 12: Instruction as Placeholder

**Before**:
- Label: "Email"
- Placeholder: "Enter your email address"

**After**:
- Label: "Email address"
- Placeholder: "name@example.com"

**Why it's better**:
- Placeholder shows example, not instruction
- Label is complete and descriptive
- More helpful for users

---

### Example 13: Password Requirements

**Before**: "Password must contain: uppercase letter, lowercase letter, number, special character, minimum 8 characters"

**After**: "Password (at least 8 characters, include a number)"

Or as helper text below the field:
"At least 8 characters and include a number"

**Why it's better**:
- Focus on the most important requirements
- Simpler requirements are easier to satisfy
- Less overwhelming for users

---

## Confirmations & Warnings

### Example 20: Vague Confirmation

**Before**: "Are you sure?"

**After**: "Delete this project? This action cannot be undone."

**Why it's better**:
- Specific about what's being deleted
- Explains consequences (cannot be undone)
- Helps users make informed decision

---

### Example 21: Unclear Consequences

**Before**: "Delete account? [Yes] [No]"

**After**: "Delete your account? All your data will be permanently deleted within 30 days. This action cannot be undone. [Cancel] [Delete permanently]"

**Why it's better**:
- Clearly explains what will happen
- Timeline for deletion (30 days)
- Emphasizes permanence
- Button labels are specific ("Delete permanently" not just "Yes")

---

### Example 22: Warning Without Context

**Before**: "WARNING: This action is irreversible"

**After**: "Deleting this file is permanent. Make sure you have a backup if you need it later."

**Why it's better**:
- Specific about what's irreversible
- Helps users understand the implication
- Suggests prevention (backup)
- Less alarming, more helpful

---
