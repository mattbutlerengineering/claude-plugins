# UX Writing Glossary

Common terms and definitions used in UX writing, content design, and product copy.

---

## A

### Accessibility
Designing products usable by people with disabilities (visual, auditory, motor, cognitive). Includes screen reader compatibility, plain language, and inclusive design.

**Example**: Using descriptive link text instead of "click here" improves accessibility for screen reader users.

---

### Active Voice
Sentence structure where the subject performs the action.

**Example**: "You saved the file" (active) vs. "The file was saved" (passive)

**Why it matters**: Active voice is more direct and easier to understand.

---

### Alt Text (Alternative Text)
Descriptive text for images, read by screen readers for visually impaired users.

**Example**: "Line graph showing revenue growth from $1M to $5M over 5 years"

---

## B

### Brevity
Using as few words as possible without losing meaning. A core principle of UX writing.

**Example**: "Save changes" is more brief than "Click here to save your changes"

---

### Button Copy
Text on clickable buttons. Should start with a verb and describe the action.

**Example**: "Download report", "Create account", "Save changes"

---

## C

### Call-to-Action (CTA)
Text or button that prompts users to take a specific action.

**Example**: "Get started", "Sign up free", "Learn more"

**Why it matters**: CTAs drive conversions and guide users through flows.

---

### Capitalization
How words are capitalized in UI text. Common styles: sentence case, title case, ALL CAPS.

**Example**:
- Sentence case: "Save changes"
- Title case: "Save Changes"
- ALL CAPS: "SAVE CHANGES" (avoid)

---

### Confirmation Message
Message shown after a user completes an action successfully.

**Example**: "Your profile has been updated", "Changes saved"

---

### Content Design
Broader term for UX writing that includes strategy, structure, and user research. Originated in UK government digital services.

---

### Conversational UI
Interface that mimics human conversation (chatbots, voice assistants).

**Example**: "How can I help you today?" instead of "Search"

---

## D

### Destructive Action
Action that permanently deletes or removes data. Requires clear warnings and confirmation.

**Example**: "Delete account", "Remove team member"

**Best practice**: Use explicit confirmation dialogs.

---

### Directional Language
Words that reference visual location (above, below, left, right).

**Why it matters**: Screen reader users can't see layout, so directional language can be confusing.

**Avoid**: "Click the button on the right"
**Better**: "Click the 'Save' button"

---

## E

### Empty State
Screen or section with no content. Good empty states explain why it's empty and encourage first action.

**Example**: "No projects yet. Create your first project to get started."

---

### Error Message
Message shown when something goes wrong or user input is invalid.

**Formula**: [What happened] + [Why] + [What to do]

**Example**: "We couldn't save your changes. Your internet connection was lost. Please reconnect and try again."

---

## F

### Filler Words
Unnecessary words that can be removed without losing meaning.

**Examples**: very, really, actually, just, simply, basically, essentially

**Before**: "Simply click the button to proceed"
**After**: "Click to proceed"

---

### Form Label
Text describing an input field.

**Example**: "Email address", "First name", "Password"

**Best practice**: Use sentence case, no colons.

---

## G

### Gender-Neutral Language
Language that doesn't assume gender.

**Avoid**: "he/she", "salesman", "chairman"
**Use**: "they/them", "salesperson", "chair"

---

## H

### Helper Text
Additional guidance shown below form fields or UI elements.

**Example**: "At least 8 characters and include a number" (below password field)

---

## I

### Imperative Mood
Grammar form used for commands or instructions. Most direct way to guide users.

**Example**: "Enter your password" (imperative) vs "You should enter your password" (not imperative)

---

### Inclusive Language
Language that respects all users regardless of ability, gender, race, or other characteristics.

**Examples**:
- Use "they" instead of "he/she"
- Avoid ableist terms like "blind to" or "crazy"
- Use person-first language: "person with disability" vs "disabled person"

---

### Inline Validation
Error or success messages shown directly next to form fields as users type.

**Example**: "This username is already taken" shown below username field

---

## J

### Jargon
Technical or specialized terms that general users may not understand.

**Example**: "Authenticate credentials" (jargon) vs "Sign in" (plain language)

---

## L

### Loading State
Screen or message shown while content is loading.

**Example**: "Loading your dashboard...", "Uploading file..."

**Best practice**: Tell users what's loading and set expectations.

---

### Localization (L10n)
Adapting product for different languages and regions.

**Considerations**: Text expansion (30-50% longer when translated), date formats, cultural references

---

## M

### Microcopy
Small bits of text in UI (button labels, error messages, form labels). Term popularized by UX writing community.

**Examples**: Placeholder text, tooltips, button labels

---

### Modal
Pop-up dialog that requires user interaction before returning to main screen.

**Example**: Confirmation dialog, sign-in prompt, settings panel

---

## O

### Onboarding
Process of introducing new users to a product and guiding them to first success.

**Example**: Account setup flow, welcome tutorial, first-time user guide

---

## P

### Passive Voice
Sentence structure where the subject receives the action.

**Example**: "The file was saved" (passive) vs "You saved the file" (active)

**When to avoid**: Most UI text (active voice is clearer)

---

### Placeholder Text
Example text shown inside an empty form field.

**Example**: "name@example.com" in email field

**Best practice**: Show examples, not instructions.

---

### Plain Language
Writing that's easy for average person to understand. Typically 8th-grade reading level.

**Principles**:
- Short sentences
- Common words
- Active voice
- No jargon

---

### Progressive Disclosure
Revealing information gradually as users need it, rather than all at once.

**Example**: Showing advanced features only after users master basics

---

## R

### Reading Level
Measure of how difficult text is to read (often using Flesch-Kincaid grade level).

**Target**: 8th-grade level for general audiences

---

### Redundancy
Unnecessary repetition of information.

**Example**: "Please enter in your email" (redundant) vs "Enter your email" (concise)

---

## S

### Screen Reader
Software that reads digital text aloud for visually impaired users.

**Examples**: JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver

**Why it matters**: UI text must work when read aloud.

---

### Second Person
Using "you/your" to address the user directly.

**Example**: "Your account" (second person) vs "The user's account" (third person)

---

### Sentence Case
Capitalization style where only the first word and proper nouns are capitalized.

**Example**: "Save changes", "Add to cart"

**Best for**: Buttons, labels, menu items (modern standard)

---

### Style Guide
Document defining voice, tone, grammar, and writing standards for a product.

**Examples**: Google Material Design, Apple HIG, Microsoft Style Guide

---

## T

### Title Case
Capitalization style where all major words are capitalized.

**Example**: "Save Changes", "Add to Cart"

**Best for**: Page titles, section headings

---

### Tone
Emotional quality of writing. How words sound to the user.

**Examples**: Friendly, professional, helpful, casual, formal

**Varies by context**: Errors should be calm and helpful; success should be positive.

---

### Tooltip
Small pop-up that appears on hover/tap, providing additional context.

**Example**: "Verified badge confirms your identity to other users"

**Best practice**: Under 150 characters, explain "why" not just "what"

---

### Truncation
Cutting off text that's too long, usually with ellipsis (...).

**Example**: "Create a new project and invite y..."

**Best practice**: Avoid truncating critical information.

---

## U

### UI Copy
All text in a user interface. Synonymous with UX writing, product copy.

---

### User Flow
Sequence of steps a user takes to complete a task.

**Example**: Sign-up flow, checkout flow, onboarding flow

---

### UX Writing
Writing copy specifically for user interfaces. Focused on clarity, brevity, and usability.

**Also called**: Content design, product writing, UI copy

---

## V

### Validation
Checking if user input meets requirements (format, length, required fields).

**Example**: Email format validation, password strength checking

---

### Voice
Brand's consistent personality and style across all writing.

**Example**: Mailchimp's voice is friendly and approachable; Microsoft's is professional and helpful.

**Note**: Voice stays consistent; tone changes by context.

---

## W

### WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)
International standards for web accessibility.

**Levels**: A (minimum), AA (standard), AAA (enhanced)

**URL**: https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/quickref/

## Sentence Structures

### Active Voice Structure
Subject + Verb + Object

**Example**: "You (subject) deleted (verb) the file (object)"

---

### Passive Voice Structure
Object + Verb + (by subject)

**Example**: "The file (object) was deleted (verb) by you (subject)"

---

### Imperative Mood Structure
Verb + Object (subject "you" is implied)

**Example**: "Delete (verb) the file (object)" — "you" is implied

---

## Measurements

### Character Limits (Guidelines)

| Element | Recommended Limit |
|---------|------------------|
| Button | <80 characters (usually 1-3 words) |
| Menu item | 1-3 words |
| Form label | 1-4 words |
| Tooltip | <150 characters |
| Error message | 1-3 sentences, <280 characters |
| Placeholder | Short example |
| Help text | 1-2 sentences |

---

### Reading Level Scores

**Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level**:
- 8th grade or lower: Easy for most audiences
- 9th-12th grade: Moderate difficulty
- College+: Too difficult for general audiences

**Flesch Reading Ease** (0-100 scale):
- 90-100: Very easy (5th grade)
- 60-70: Fairly easy (8th-9th grade)
- 30-50: Difficult (college level)
- 0-30: Very difficult (graduate level)

---

## Formula References

### Error Message Formula
[What happened] + [Why it happened] + [What to do next]

**Example**: "We couldn't save your changes. Your internet connection was lost. Please reconnect and try again."

---

### Onboarding Formula
Welcome → Set expectations → Guide step by step → Celebrate completion → Encourage next action

---

### Button Label Formula
[Verb] + [Object]

**Examples**: "Save changes", "Delete account", "Download report"

---

## Related Disciplines

### Content Strategy
Planning, creation, and governance of content. Broader than UX writing.

### Information Architecture (IA)
Organization and structure of content. Includes navigation labels.

### Usability Writing
Writing focused on making products easy to use. Overlaps with UX writing.

### Technical Writing
Writing documentation and technical content. Related but distinct from UX writing.
